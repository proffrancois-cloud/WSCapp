Going Pains

Map of the guide

This guide keeps the app's route plan, but it reads Going Pains as one connected lesson for strong middle-school readers using B1 English. The guide slows down the examples so students can see the people, places, objects, dates, and choices inside each entry.

There are 6 official entries in this section: Adolescence Is Old. Teenagers Are New., Growing Up Has Different Names, Rites, Rules, and the Moment You “Become” an Adult, Growing Up Is Messy, “Adulting”: Acting Like a Grown-Up, and The Last Threshold. Read them as a curriculum unit. Each entry gives one case, but the guide asks how the cases speak to each other.

The rewrite also checks the outside guide notes and resource packets, including Are We There Yet IGNITION, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 1, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 2, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 3, Intro Questions, other section notes. Those notes are used to add background, not to replace the official raw content.

The big route of the section

This section is about growing through change. It starts with adolescence, but it also studies names, rituals, culture, adult tasks, art, and death as different kinds of threshold.

The official WSC text gives the short version, but the linked sources and student explanations give the real working material. When an entry includes an article, video, poem, artwork, or historical case, treat that link as part of the syllabus.

Why these examples belong together

The examples belong together because growing up is not one clean moment. Biology, language, school, money, family, art, and law all help decide when a person seems to have moved from one stage to another.

Core concepts explained from zero

These concepts are the basic tools for the section. They are simple on purpose. A student should be able to use each one in a short answer before moving to harder comparisons.

At the entry level, this helps students understand why adulthood can feel like a performance. At the section level, it modernizes Going Pains by showing how culture jokes about the gap between responsibility and identity.

Chapter 2 - Adolescence as unstable status

The idea

The chapter moves from Adolescence Is Old. Teenagers Are New. to Growing Up Has Different Names, and Rites, Rules, and the Moment You “Become” an Adult. The route is not random: it starts with this point: Adolescence is ancient, but teenage identity is modern. Then it tests that idea through Growing Up Has Different Names and ends by asking how Rites, Rules, and the Moment You “Become” an Adult changes the pattern.

The reading skill here is to connect a fact to a pattern. A student should be able to explain the case, then say what it teaches about Going Pains.

The official WSC examples

Adolescence Is Old. Teenagers Are New. begins from this official problem: In 2001, millennial pop star Britney Spears sang that she was “not a girl, not yet a woman”. In other words, she was an adolescent. Actually, she was 20 at the time—no longer a teenager, but well below the age at which our frontal lobes stop developing. In simpler terms, Adolescence is not just a modern human invention. The UCLA links show that many animals also pass through a risky, flexible learning stage between childhood and adulthood, when they explore, leave safe places, learn social rules, and test themselves. The newer part is the teenager as a social identity: a visible age group shaped by school, youth culture, spending money, music, media, and modern expectations. At the entry level, this separates three ideas students often mix together: puberty, adolescence, and teenager identity. At the section level, it opens Going Pains by showing that growing up is biological, social, linguistic, and cultural all at once.

Growing Up Has Different Names begins from this official problem: The word “adolescent” comes from a Latin word meaning “to grow toward”. But “teenager” relies on a quirk of the English language—that the numbers 11 and 12 have their own unique words, while 13 through 19 are compound words that combine a single-digit number... In simpler terms, The word adolescent points toward growth: it comes from a root meaning to grow or grow toward maturity. The word teenager works differently: it depends on the English number pattern from thirteen to nineteen. That means the two words overlap, but they do not carry the same feeling. Adolescent sounds developmental or scientific; teenager sounds more social and cultural. Other languages divide the age range differently, which reminds us that life stages are partly biological and partly shaped by language. At the entry level, this helps students notice that words do not just label growing up; they shape how people imagine it. At the section level, it adds language and culture to the science of adolescence.

Rites, Rules, and the Moment You “Become” an Adult begins from this official problem: Many cultures hold special ceremonies to celebrate the passage from the dependency of childhood to the responsibilities of adulthood. In simpler terms, Growing up is usually gradual, but many communities create special moments that say: now something has changed. Some rites of passage are cultural or religious, like a quinceañera, Seijin no Hi, or bar/bat mitzvah. Others are secular or legal, like graduation, voting, a driver’s license, national service, or moving out. The important point is that a rite turns a slow and messy transition into a visible social moment that other people recognize. At the entry level, this shows that adulthood is not only biology; it is also marked by symbols, ceremonies, rights, and responsibilities. At the section level, it gives a social answer to the science of adolescence: rites of passage make change legible.

8. Final synthesis

The big tensions

The main tension in Going Pains is that people want clear arrivals, but real life gives mixed routes. The section asks students to hold both sides at once: movement can be useful and costly, waiting can be empty and meaningful, and an ending can be official without feeling complete.

How the examples connect

The examples connect through a chain. Start with Adolescence Is Old. Teenagers Are New., then compare it with Growing Up Has Different Names, Rites, Rules, and the Moment You “Become” an Adult, and Growing Up Is Messy. From there, bring in the later entries to show how the same pressure changes when the scale moves from a person to a group, a city, a technology, a story, or a whole society.

Pitfalls to avoid

A basic answer treats each entry as a separate trivia item. A stronger answer asks what job the example does inside the section, how it changes the route question, and why WSC placed it near the other examples.

What alpacas should remember

Alpacas should remember the section as a set of tools, not as a list of facts. For every entry, be ready to say what is moving, what is delayed, who benefits, who pays, what sign of progress appears, and what kind of arrival is being questioned.
