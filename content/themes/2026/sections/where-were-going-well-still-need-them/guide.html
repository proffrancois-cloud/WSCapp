Where We’re Going, We’ll Still Need Them

Map of the guide

This guide keeps the app's route plan, but it reads Where We're Going, We'll Still Need Them as one connected lesson for strong middle-school readers using B1 English. The guide slows down the examples so students can see the people, places, objects, dates, and choices inside each entry.

There are 11 official entries in this section: Roads Before Roads Became Obvious, Roads That Think, Charge, Heal, and Drain, Shortcuts, Scenic Routes, and the Logic of the Best Path, Why Stroads Fail, The Road as a State of Mind, Why the Road Trip Became American, Can a Bus Journey Be a Road Trip?, Why Route 66 Still Matters, Motels: Sleeping on the Road, Songs, Paintings, and the Feeling of the Highway, and Circles, Detours, and Why a Journey Matters. Read them as a curriculum unit. Each entry gives one case, but the guide asks how the cases speak to each other.

The rewrite also checks the outside guide notes and resource packets, including Are We There Yet IGNITION, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 1, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 2, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 3, Intro Questions, other section notes. Those notes are used to add background, not to replace the official raw content.

The big route of the section

This section argues that future movement still depends on ordinary ground systems. Roads, paths, materials, maintenance, bus routes, motels, songs, paintings, and road myths all show that infrastructure and imagination travel together.

The official WSC text gives the short version, but the linked sources and student explanations give the real working material. When an entry includes an article, video, poem, artwork, or historical case, treat that link as part of the syllabus.

Why these examples belong together

The examples belong together because the road is both a physical object and a cultural idea. People build roads to move, but they also use roads to tell stories about freedom, danger, identity, and national memory.

Core concepts explained from zero

These concepts are the basic tools for the section. They are simple on purpose. A student should be able to use each one in a short answer before moving to harder comparisons.

The official WSC examples

Roads Before Roads Became Obvious begins from this official problem: “All roads lead to Rome,” insisted one medieval European proverb. (In other parts of the world, roads led elsewhere.) While Roman roads may be the most famous for their longevity and scale, they were far from the first road network. In simpler terms, Roads did not begin with Rome, but Roman roads became famous because they made movement durable, repeatable, and politically useful across huge distances. This entry asks students to see roads as more than pavement: a road can be engineered from stone, formed from logs, worn into a hillside, marked by milestones, or remembered as a legendary route. Roads are technologies of movement, but also technologies of power, trade, memory, and control. At the entry level, this helps students understand that roads are many different solutions to the same problem: how to make movement possible again and again. At the section level, it opens Where We’re Going, We’ll Still Need Them by showing why future movement still depends on old physical routes.

The Road as a State of Mind begins from this official problem: “It’s a dangerous business, Frodo, going out your door,” says Bilbo to his nephew in The Fellowship of the Ring, and he doesn’t even have stroads to worry about. In simpler terms, In these works, the road is not just a surface under someone’s feet. It becomes longing, tiredness, freedom, exile, duty, temptation, memory, or fear. Some speakers choose movement; others are pushed by loss, duty, age, or restlessness. The road can promise adventure, but it can also reveal loneliness and the cost of leaving home. The poems make the road psychological. At the entry level, this helps students read roads as emotional symbols. At the section level, it shifts the guiding section from engineering to inner life. At the year-theme level, it connects to home, wandering, and thresholds because leaving the doorstep always means entering uncertainty.

Why the Road Trip Became American begins from this official problem: While the first long-distance car journey took place in Germany, it was in America that the concept of the road trip took hold. Read more about its history then discuss with your team: why is the idea of the road trip so associated with America even when... In simpler terms, Bertha Benz’s 1888 journey in Germany helped prove that the automobile could be useful beyond short demonstrations. But the road trip became especially powerful in the United States because roads, cars, distance, tourism, migration, and ideas of freedom all came together. The American road trip is not just a drive; it became a national story about space, independence, discovery, and self-direction. At the entry level, this helps students separate the first long-distance car journey from the later road-trip myth. At the section level, it shows how infrastructure becomes culture.

Why Route 66 Still Matters begins from this official problem: The Federal Highway Administration of the United States calls it “the Mother Road”: others refer to it as the Main Street of America. Though no longer a major thoroughfare, Route 66 is still widely revered and remembered. In simpler terms, Route 66 is not famous today because it is the fastest way across the United States. It matters because it became a cultural route: a symbol of migration, car travel, diners, gas stations, motels, small towns, music, and an older dream of America on the move. It shows how a road can stop being essential infrastructure and still remain powerful in memory. Route 66 matters after usefulness because it became a memory route. At the entry level, this helps students see how roads become symbols. At the section level, it shifts from road function to road mythology. At the year-theme level, it connects to journeys and destinations because some places matter as stories even after they stop being the most practical route.

Shortcuts, Scenic Routes, and the Logic of the Best Path begins from this official problem: Urban designers know the frustration of watching unofficial trails sprout on perfectly manicured lawns. These are desire paths: the physical evidence of the shortest distance between two points. In simpler terms, A desire path appears when people repeatedly choose the route that feels most natural, even if planners expected them to walk somewhere else. This makes shortcuts a kind of evidence: they show what people actually do, not just what designs told them to do. But the shortest route is not always the best one. Real route choices can balance speed, safety, energy, uncertainty, learning, crowding, and purpose. This entry should compare optimization with human preference. At the entry level, this helps students question the idea that efficiency always means straightness. At the section level, it shifts from roads as objects to routes as choices.

Why Stroads Fail begins from this official problem: If the chicken crossed the stroad, it would probably get run over. Explore what makes stroads different from—and, according to critics, more dangerous than—traditional streets and roads, then discuss with your team: are there any in your community, and are... In simpler terms, A stroad is a hybrid that tries to be a fast road and a local street at the same time. That sounds efficient, but critics argue it often fails at both: cars move too fast for a place full of driveways, shops, and pedestrians, while the space feels too dangerous and unpleasant to work as a real street. A stroad is a design problem because it mixes purposes that pull against each other. A stroad fails because it tries to be a fast road and a productive street at the same time. At the entry level, this helps students understand why some ordinary-looking roads feel stressful or unsafe. At the section level, it adds a real urban-design failure to the discussion of routes and infrastructure.

Can a Bus Journey Be a Road Trip? begins from this official problem: Be sure to look into other long distance overland journeys, including the once-legendary London to Calcutta bus service and the still operational Transoceánica from Brazil to Peru. Can they be called road trips even if they don’t take place in cars? In simpler terms, Long overland bus journeys show that road travel does not belong only to private cars. A bus from London to Calcutta or a route across South America can be epic, exhausting, and transformative. Motels: Sleeping on the Road begins from this official problem: People on road trips needed a place to sleep at night. Early on, many simply pulled off the road and camped beside their cars; over time, a new kind of car-friendly hotel emerged to serve them. In simpler terms, When cars made long-distance road travel more common, travelers needed places to sleep without leaving the road world behind. The motel emerged as a motor hotel: a car-friendly form of lodging designed for easy access, parking, short stays, and continued movement. It is not simply a smaller hotel; it is architecture shaped by road culture. The motel is architecture shaped by the car: parking near the room, easy highway access, short stays, and a promise that sleep is only a pause before more movement. At the entry level, this shows how movement creates new building types. At the section level, it extends road culture from driving to stopping. At the year-theme level, it connects to home and wandering because motels are classic in-between spaces: places to pause without fully arriving.

8. Final synthesis

The big tensions

The main tension in Where We're Going, We'll Still Need Them is that people want clear arrivals, but real life gives mixed routes. The section asks students to hold both sides at once: movement can be useful and costly, waiting can be empty and meaningful, and an ending can be official without feeling complete.

How the examples connect

The examples connect through a chain. Start with Roads Before Roads Became Obvious, then compare it with Roads That Think, Charge, Heal, and Drain, Shortcuts, Scenic Routes, and the Logic of the Best Path, and Why Stroads Fail. From there, bring in the later entries to show how the same pressure changes when the scale moves from a person to a group, a city, a technology, a story, or a whole society.

Pitfalls to avoid

A basic answer treats each entry as a separate trivia item. A stronger answer asks what job the example does inside the section, how it changes the route question, and why WSC placed it near the other examples.

What alpacas should remember

Alpacas should remember the section as a set of tools, not as a list of facts. For every entry, be ready to say what is moving, what is delayed, who benefits, who pays, what sign of progress appears, and what kind of arrival is being questioned.
