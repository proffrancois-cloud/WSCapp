Home and Wandering

Map of the guide

This guide keeps the app's route plan, but it reads Home and Wandering as one connected lesson for strong middle-school readers using B1 English. The guide slows down the examples so students can see the people, places, objects, dates, and choices inside each entry.

There are 8 official entries in this section: Finding the Sea: Tools That Made the Ocean Measurable, How Animals Find Their Way, Wayfinding Without Screens, Should Some Parts of the World Stay Foggy?, Stuck in Transit, Reaching the Border Is Not the Same as Being Accepted, Poems of Migration and In-Between Lives, and Art, Music, and the Migrant In-Between. Read them as a curriculum unit. Each entry gives one case, but the guide asks how the cases speak to each other.

The rewrite also checks the outside guide notes and resource packets, including Are We There Yet IGNITION, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 1, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 2, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 3, Intro Questions, other section notes. Those notes are used to add background, not to replace the official raw content.

The big route of the section

This section studies movement without treating the traveler as a dot on a map. Home, wandering, navigation, animal movement, borders, poetry, and migrant art all ask how people know where they are and where they belong.

The official WSC text gives the short version, but the linked sources and student explanations give the real working material. When an entry includes an article, video, poem, artwork, or historical case, treat that link as part of the syllabus.

Why these examples belong together

The examples belong together because movement is both physical and emotional. A compass can measure direction, an animal can sense a route, an airport can trap a person, and a poem can hold the pain of divided belonging.

Core concepts explained from zero

These concepts are the basic tools for the section. They are simple on purpose. A student should be able to use each one in a short answer before moving to harder comparisons.

Chapter 1 - Home as more than location

The idea

The chapter moves from Stuck in Transit to Reaching the Border Is Not the Same as Being Accepted, Poems of Migration and In-Between Lives, and Art, Music, and the Migrant In-Between. The route is not random: it starts with this point: A transit space can become a suspended home when law, paperwork, and politics block arrival. Then it tests that idea through Reaching the Border Is Not the Same as Being Accepted and ends by asking how Art, Music, and the Migrant In-Between changes the pattern.

The reading skill here is to connect a fact to a pattern. The main pattern is Movement Without Arrival, Infrastructure Shapes Behavior, Narrated Endings; the subject doorway is Politics & Government / Global Politics, Geography / Human Geography, Language and Literature, Visual and Performing Arts. A student should be able to explain the case, then say what it teaches about Home and Wandering.

The official WSC examples

Stuck in Transit begins from this official problem: In the 2004 film The Terminal, a traveler lands at an American airport only to discover that his country’s government has collapsed. In simpler terms, The Terminal turns an airport into a home, prison, and border all at once: a traveler can move around the terminal but cannot truly enter or leave. The real case of Mehran Karimi Nasseri and the Reuters story about Edward Snowden make the idea more serious. Airport limbo is not just inconvenience; it can happen when passports, asylum, state collapse, and international pressure trap someone between legal systems. At the entry level, this shows how a place built for movement can become a place of suspension. At the section level, it gives Home and Wandering a clear liminal case: neither home nor destination, neither arrival nor departure.

Reaching the Border Is Not the Same as Being Accepted begins from this official problem: Immigrants by land and sea can also face obstacles to entry when they reach their destinations. Consider the case of the St. Louis, a ship carrying Jewish refugees from Nazi Germany in 1939. In simpler terms, Arriving at a border is not the same as being welcomed through it. The St. Louis case shows this in the most tragic way: people fleeing Nazi Germany reached possible safety, but the United States, Canada, and Cuba refused them, and many later died in the Holocaust. Modern asylum and refugee debates are different in context, but they raise the same threshold question: when people arrive in danger, how do states balance law, capacity, fear, responsibility, and human need? The St. At the entry level, this shows that borders are decisions, not just map lines. At the section level, it deepens Home and Wandering by showing that wandering can end at a closed threshold. At the year-theme level, it asks whether “getting there” matters if the destination refuses to let you belong.

Poems of Migration and In-Between Lives begins from this official problem: Consider the following poems that speak to the immigrant experience. Are they expressing something universal, or are they too constrained by the specific experience of migrating to the United States? In simpler terms, These poems show migration from the inside: hope, warning, memory, ancestry, language, loss, and the strange feeling of being between homes. The links matter because each poem gives a different version of the threshold. Lazarus imagines welcome, Rich warns that crossing changes you, Chin explores becoming American while carrying older histories, Castro focuses on voice and language, and Blanco turns exile into family memory. At the entry level, this helps students see how poetry can hold complicated migration feelings in a small space. At the section level, it gives Home and Wandering an emotional and literary voice after the policy and navigation entries.

Art, Music, and the Migrant In-Between begins from this official problem: Some artists, too, explore the liminality of the migrant. Whether between home and the unfamiliar, or legal and “alien” status, how does each of them treat the state of being in-between? In simpler terms, These works show the migrant in-between through images, panels, rhythm, theater, protest, and memory. Jacob Lawrence’s Migration Series is the strongest visual anchor because it turns mass movement into a 60-panel story of collective displacement and hope. The songs add emotional range: leaving home, crossing borders, being labeled “alien,” remembering Ellis Island, or insisting that identity cannot be reduced to paperwork. At the entry level, this helps students compare how different art forms make migration visible and audible. At the section level, it closes Home and Wandering by moving from routes and policies to identity and expression.

The idea

The official WSC examples

Finding the Sea: Tools That Made the Ocean Measurable begins from this official problem: In the fifteenth century, Europeans launched their caravels and galleons on what would become a merciless colonial crusade around the globe. In simpler terms, European ocean expansion depended on instruments that turned the sea into something measurable: direction, speed, time, position, and coordinates. The compass link matters because it shows that the compass was not just a needle pointing north; sailors also had to understand magnetic variation, ship interference, and how navigation tools changed the world. At the entry level, this shows that leaving home across the ocean required tools, measurements, and learned systems. At the section level, it opens Home and Wandering by asking how humans convert frightening unknown space into routes.

How Animals Find Their Way begins from this official problem: We have Google Maps, or Baidu, but how do other animals know when they’ve arrived? From the monarch butterfly’s multigenerational migration to the globe-trotting of the humpback whale, animals follow astoundingly complex routes around the Earth. In simpler terms, Animals navigate through many overlapping systems: magnetic sensing, stars, polarized light, smells, echoes, landmarks, spatial memory, and restless migration behavior. The links are important because each example shows a different kind of “map”: indigo buntings use the night sky, sea turtles use magnetic patterns, bees and ants use landmarks and path integration, and pigeons combine several cues. At the entry level, this shows students that navigation is not only a human invention. At the section level, it expands Home and Wandering from ships and maps to living bodies moving across the planet.

Wayfinding Without Screens begins from this official problem: “We know where we are,” sang Moana’s ancestors—and they knew it without maps, compasses, or access to Starlink. Instead, Polynesians drew on a complex system of non-instrument navigation, including the star compass, ocean swells, and animal sightings. In simpler terms, Wayfinding without screens is not guessing. Polynesian navigators read stars, swells, winds, birds, clouds, and memory across huge ocean distances, while Inuit travelers read snow, ice, sound, currents, cairns, animal movement, and story-linked landmarks in the Arctic. The links matter because they show navigation as a relationship with the world: the map can live in the body, memory, elders, and environment, not only on a phone. At the entry level, this helps students respect traditional navigation as skilled knowledge rather than “primitive” travel. At the section level, it deepens Home and Wandering by contrasting device-based navigation with attention-based navigation.

Should Some Parts of the World Stay Foggy? begins from this official problem: In some video games, such as Zelda and Yakuza, the unexplored regions of the map are cloudy until you visit them. The real world used to be more like that: Columbus sailed into the unknown (and never reached his intended “there”) and you couldn’t know what... In simpler terms, Fog of World turns real travel into something closer to game exploration: places stay hidden until you physically visit them. The link matters because the app does not promise better efficiency; it promises a richer experience. That raises the entry’s real question: do maps help us by reducing uncertainty, or can too much certainty make the world feel pre-spoiled before we arrive? At the entry level, this asks whether knowledge always improves experience. At the section level, it shifts Home and Wandering from navigation accuracy to the feeling of discovery.

8. Final synthesis

The big tensions

The main tension in Home and Wandering is that people want clear arrivals, but real life gives mixed routes. The section asks students to hold both sides at once: movement can be useful and costly, waiting can be empty and meaningful, and an ending can be official without feeling complete.

How the examples connect

The examples connect through a chain. Start with Finding the Sea: Tools That Made the Ocean Measurable, then compare it with How Animals Find Their Way, Wayfinding Without Screens, and Should Some Parts of the World Stay Foggy?. From there, bring in the later entries to show how the same pressure changes when the scale moves from a person to a group, a city, a technology, a story, or a whole society.

Pitfalls to avoid

A basic answer treats each entry as a separate trivia item. A stronger answer asks what job the example does inside the section, how it changes the route question, and why WSC placed it near the other examples.

What alpacas should remember

Alpacas should remember the section as a set of tools, not as a list of facts. For every entry, be ready to say what is moving, what is delayed, who benefits, who pays, what sign of progress appears, and what kind of arrival is being questioned.
