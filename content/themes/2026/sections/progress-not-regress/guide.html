Progress, Not Regress

Map of the guide

This guide keeps the app's route plan, but it reads Progress, Not Regress as one connected lesson for strong middle-school readers using B1 English. The guide slows down the examples so students can see the people, places, objects, dates, and choices inside each entry.

There are 8 official entries in this section: The History of the Progress Bar, Brad A. Myers, Progress Bar, So Many of Them!, Placebo Buttons, Feeling Faker, The Airport Baggage Claim Trick, Effect of Progress Indicators on Individuals, Play to Wait, How Can Societies Measure Progress?, and Is the World on Its Way to a Better or Worse Place?. Read them as a curriculum unit. Each entry gives one case, but the guide asks how the cases speak to each other.

The rewrite also checks the outside guide notes and resource packets, including Are We There Yet IGNITION, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 1, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 2, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 3, Intro Questions, other section notes. Those notes are used to add background, not to replace the official raw content.

The big route of the section

This section asks how people know whether they are moving forward. Progress can be a real change, a number on a chart, a bar on a screen, or only a feeling created by design.

The official WSC text gives the short version, but the linked sources and student explanations give the real working material. When an entry includes an article, video, poem, artwork, or historical case, treat that link as part of the syllabus.

Why these examples belong together

The examples belong together because they show that progress is never only a fact. Interfaces, airport design, games, social indicators, and world data all shape what people believe about movement toward a goal.

Core concepts explained from zero

These concepts are the basic tools for the section. They are simple on purpose. A student should be able to use each one in a short answer before moving to harder comparisons.

Chapter 1 - Waiting that has a shape

The idea

The chapter moves from The History of the Progress Bar, Brad A. Myers to Progress Bar, So Many of Them!, and Effect of Progress Indicators on Individuals. The route is not random: it starts with this point: Progress can be something users need to feel, not only something systems measure. Then it tests that idea through Progress Bar, So Many of Them! and ends by asking how Effect of Progress Indicators on Individuals changes the pattern.

The reading skill here is to connect a fact to a pattern. The main pattern is Performed Progress, Delayed Arrival, Visible vs Real; the subject doorway is Computer Science / Technology, Psychology. A student should be able to explain the case, then say what it teaches about Progress, Not Regress.

The official WSC examples

The History of the Progress Bar, Brad A. Myers begins from this official problem: Before 1985, you'd have had no idea how long it would take to install a program on your computer - or to save a file, or to complete an online form. (To be fair, there were no online forms. In simpler terms, In early personal computing, waiting could feel like failure: users often had no clear way to know whether a program was working, frozen, or about to crash. Brad A. Myers's research on percent-done progress indicators showed that a visible sign of progress could make waiting feel more acceptable. The most important insight is that people were not only asking, 'Is the computer done?' They were asking, 'Is anything happening at all?' A progress bar answers that emotional question, even when its timing is imperfect. At the entry level, this shows that progress is partly psychological: humans tolerate delays better when they can see movement. At the section level, it introduces Progress, Not Regress by showing that waiting can be designed, measured, and even manipulated.

Progress Bar, So Many of Them! begins from this official problem: Bars are not the only way to pass time. Explore the following alternatives to the standard progress bar, then discuss with your team: what are the advantages and disadvantages of each approach? In simpler terms, Not every waiting interface is a progress bar. Some indicators show a percentage, some only signal that work is happening, and some distract or prepare the user while content loads. These patterns all answer the same emotional problem in different ways: users dislike being left in the dark. A good loading pattern does not merely decorate a delay; it tells the user what kind of delay they are experiencing. The varieties should be explained by what kind of uncertainty they solve. At the entry level, this helps students compare different interface solutions to waiting. At the section level, it expands progress from one famous bar into a whole design language of perceived movement.

Effect of Progress Indicators on Individuals begins from this official problem: Designers have discovered that progress bars in apps make users more likely to complete tasks such as surveys and applications. Discuss with your team: is it okay to design interfaces that persuade people to complete actions? In simpler terms, Progress indicators can change whether people continue or quit. In surveys, applications, games, and loyalty systems, users often respond not only to the task itself but to the feeling that they are getting somewhere. Research on progress feedback shows that visible progress can encourage completion when it feels motivating, but it can also backfire if progress looks slow or discouraging. At the entry level, this helps students understand that progress design can shape behavior. At the section level, it turns waiting indicators into persuasion tools.

The official WSC examples

Placebo Buttons, Feeling Faker begins from this official problem: Some progress bars continue to inch along even when a process is actually stuck; the idea is to encourage people not to give up. Explore the idea of placebo buttons - such as the 'close door' buttons on elevators that almost never close the doors, or... In simpler terms, A placebo button is a control that feels meaningful even when it has little or no effect on the system. Some elevator close-door buttons, some crosswalk buttons, and even dummy thermostats have been discussed this way. The key idea is not simply that buttons are fake, but that people feel calmer when they can do something. Placebo buttons turn waiting from helpless passivity into an action, even if the action does not truly change the result. At the entry level, this helps students understand the difference between real control and perceived control. At the section level, it complicates progress by showing that people may accept waiting better when they believe they are influencing it.

The Airport Baggage Claim Trick begins from this official problem: Some schemes can be quite elaborate. For instance, one airport discovered that, if planes parked further away so that people had to walk longer to baggage claim, they complained less about waiting for their bags once they got there. In simpler terms, The Houston baggage-claim story shows that people do not experience all waiting the same way. Standing still at a carousel feels different from walking toward baggage, even if the total time is similar. The airport did not simply make luggage arrive faster; it changed the middle of the journey so passengers felt active rather than stuck. In other words, people often prefer visible movement to passive waiting, even if the total time to reach the destination is the same. At the entry level, this helps students understand that waiting depends on design, not only duration. At the section level, it moves the idea of progress from screens to physical space. At the year-theme level, it shows that the route itself can change whether we feel close to arrival.

Play to Wait begins from this official problem: In 1994, the developers of Namco's Ridge Racer added an Easter egg to their loading screens - a version of the studio's classic Galaxian game. Google Chrome does something similar: its Dinosaur Game appears when your device goes offline temporarily. In simpler terms, Auxiliary games turn waiting into a small side activity. Ridge Racer famously let players play Galaxian during loading, Chrome's Dino game turns internet failure into a mini runner, and some restaurants now offer games on table terminals while customers wait. These examples show a shift from showing progress to filling time. The user is not necessarily closer to the end, but the wait becomes more active, playful, or profitable. Loading games show that waiting can be converted into play. At the entry level, this helps students understand that waiting can be transformed into entertainment. At the section level, it expands progress design beyond bars and buttons into play.

The official WSC examples

How Can Societies Measure Progress? begins from this official problem: You can't know if you're there yet without some way to measure progress; the same is true for entire societies. Learn about the following socioeconomic indicators with your team: what do they try to measure? In simpler terms, Societies need progress indicators too. Chapter 4 - Social progress without inevitability

The idea

The chapter begins with Is the World on Its Way to a Better or Worse Place?. Its job is to turn one case into a tool: Whether human progress is real, automatic, fragile, or partly imagined.

The reading skill here is to connect a fact to a pattern. The main pattern is Narrated Endings, Managing Uncertainty; the subject doorway is Philosophy, History. A student should be able to explain the case, then say what it teaches about Progress, Not Regress.

The official WSC examples

Is the World on Its Way to a Better or Worse Place? begins from this official problem: The Second Law of Thermodynamics suggests that closed systems always grow more disordered over time. Left to themselves, things get messier: your room won't clean itself. In simpler terms, This entry contrasts two ideas of time. The Second Law of Thermodynamics gives a simple image of decline: left alone, systems tend toward disorder. But modern societies often imagine history as progress: more knowledge, more justice, better health, better rights, and a better future. That optimistic idea is not universal or timeless. Some cultures imagined history as cyclical, controlled by higher powers, or outside human influence. At the entry level, this helps students ask whether progress is natural, chosen, measured, or imagined. At the section level, it closes Progress, Not Regress by questioning the assumption that forward movement is always improvement.

8. Final synthesis

The big tensions

The main tension in Progress, Not Regress is that people want clear arrivals, but real life gives mixed routes. The section asks students to hold both sides at once: movement can be useful and costly, waiting can be empty and meaningful, and an ending can be official without feeling complete.

How the examples connect

The examples connect through a chain. Start with The History of the Progress Bar, Brad A. Myers, then compare it with Progress Bar, So Many of Them!, Placebo Buttons, Feeling Faker, and The Airport Baggage Claim Trick. From there, bring in the later entries to show how the same pressure changes when the scale moves from a person to a group, a city, a technology, a story, or a whole society.

Pitfalls to avoid

A basic answer treats each entry as a separate trivia item. A stronger answer asks what job the example does inside the section, how it changes the route question, and why WSC placed it near the other examples.

What alpacas should remember

Alpacas should remember the section as a set of tools, not as a list of facts. For every entry, be ready to say what is moving, what is delayed, who benefits, who pays, what sign of progress appears, and what kind of arrival is being questioned.
