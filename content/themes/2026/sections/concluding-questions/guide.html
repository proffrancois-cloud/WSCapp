Concluding Questions

Map of the guide

This guide keeps the app's route plan, but it reads Concluding Questions as one connected lesson for strong middle-school readers using B1 English. The guide slows down the examples so students can see the people, places, objects, dates, and choices inside each entry.

There are 7 official entries in this section: When Is It Wise to Let Go?, Would You Want to Know the Exact Waiting Time?, Zeno and the Problem of Ever Getting There, Patience: A Virtue, a Strategy, or a Burden?, The End of History—or Just Another Delay?, When Stories Refuse to End Clearly, and We’re There. What Comes After?. Read them as a curriculum unit. Each entry gives one case, but the guide asks how the cases speak to each other.

The rewrite also checks the outside guide notes and resource packets, including Are We There Yet IGNITION, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 1, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 2, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 3, Intro Questions, other section notes. Those notes are used to add background, not to replace the official raw content.

The big route of the section

This final section asks what endings do to people. It studies stopping, knowing the wait, never quite arriving, patience, history, unclear stories, and the strange question that comes after arrival.

The official WSC text gives the short version, but the linked sources and student explanations give the real working material. When an entry includes an article, video, poem, artwork, or historical case, treat that link as part of the syllabus.

Why these examples belong together

The examples belong together because they all challenge the easy idea that arrival solves everything. Sometimes it is wise to stop, sometimes information changes the wait, sometimes the finish line keeps moving, and sometimes a story or a society refuses to close neatly.

Core concepts explained from zero

These concepts are the basic tools for the section. They are simple on purpose. A student should be able to use each one in a short answer before moving to harder comparisons.

ConceptSimple meaningHow to use it
ClosureThe feeling that something has ended in a complete way.Ask whether the ending is official, emotional, moral, or only narrative.
Sunk costThe pressure to continue because time, effort, or money was already spent.Use it when quitting may be wiser than continuing.
PatienceThe ability to wait or continue without losing control.Ask whether patience is a virtue, a strategy, or a burden.
AftermathWhat happens after people say they have arrived.Use it to show that arrival often opens a new route.

Chapter 1 - Closure versus continuation

The idea

The chapter moves from The End of History—or Just Another Delay? to When Stories Refuse to End Clearly, and We’re There. What Comes After?. The route is not random: it starts with this point: A political ending can be inspiring but partial and reversible. Then it tests that idea through When Stories Refuse to End Clearly and ends by asking how We’re There. What Comes After? changes the pattern.

The reading skill here is to connect a fact to a pattern. The main pattern is Narrated Endings, Delayed Arrival, Thresholds & Irreversibility, Movement Without Arrival; the subject doorway is Philosophy, Language and Literature, Media / Film. A student should be able to explain the case, then say what it teaches about Concluding Questions.

The official WSC examples

The End of History—or Just Another Delay? begins from this official problem: World War I was “the War to End All Wars”; 1989 was, per Francis Fukuyama, “The End of History”. The first meant achieving global peace; the second trumpeted the triumph of liberal democracy. In simpler terms, This entry compares two huge historical hopes: the hope that World War I would end major war, and the belief that liberal democracy had won the main ideological struggle after the Cold War. Neither claim means that history literally stopped. Instead, both imagine a final destination: a world beyond war, or a political system beyond serious rivals. The question is whether humanity should aim for such endpoints, or whether history is too unstable for final arrival. At the entry level, this helps students understand two famous claims about historical endings. At the section level, it moves from personal choices and waiting to global political hopes.

When Stories Refuse to End Clearly begins from this official problem: I choose to believe. Endings aren’t always clear. In the final episode of Stargate Universe, a starship flies off into the vast emptiness between galaxies, its fate never to be resolved; cancellation left its crew’s future ambiguous. In simpler terms, Some stories refuse to end with full certainty. Sometimes that happens because a show is cancelled before it can finish. Sometimes the uncertainty is deliberate, as with The Sopranos cut to black or Life of Pi asking which version of the story we prefer. Blade Runner adds uncertainty about identity itself, while the Harry Potter epilogue shows the opposite risk: an ending can feel too neat. This entry asks whether stories need closure, or whether an ending can be powerful because it leaves us still thinking. At the entry level, this helps students compare unfinished, ambiguous, and over-closed endings. At the section level, it shifts the final questions from history and philosophy into narrative.

We’re There. What Comes After? begins from this official problem: This ending is clear: we’re there. Where do we go next? In simpler terms, The theme ends by finally saying the words students have been waiting for: we’re there. But the next sentence immediately opens a new journey. That is the point: arrival can be real, but it rarely ends thinking, growth, or movement. A finished route can become a beginning, and a destination can become the place where the next question starts. Treat this as the final pivot of the whole theme: arrival is real, but it is not the same as disappearance of motion. At the entry level, this closes the theme with a simple but powerful reversal. At the section level, it turns concluding questions into a doorway toward what comes next. At the year-theme level, it connects to journeys and endings because the final answer to “Are we there yet?” is not just yes; it is also “what now?”

Chapter 2 - Perseverance and quitting

The idea

The chapter puts When Is It Wise to Let Go? beside Patience: A Virtue, a Strategy, or a Burden?. The second case adds a second point: Waiting is wise only when it supports a better response.

The official WSC examples

When Is It Wise to Let Go? begins from this official problem: When is it a good idea to give up? Be sure to learn about the sunk cost fallacy. Does it imply that we should quit on things more often? How about on people? In simpler terms, The sunk cost fallacy happens when people keep investing time, money, effort, or emotion into something mainly because they have already invested so much. That can make quitting feel like wasting the past, even when continuing would create more stress or harm. This entry asks students to separate wise persistence from irrational attachment: sometimes “not giving up” is courage, but sometimes letting go is the clearest decision. At the entry level, this helps students understand that stopping can be a serious decision, not just failure. At the section level, it turns the theme’s final questions toward judgment: when is a destination no longer worth the route?

Patience: A Virtue, a Strategy, or a Burden? begins from this official problem: “Are we there yet?” is a question linked with impatience. Explore the psychology of patience: what makes some people willing to calmly wait a little longer? Is patience more valued in some cultures than in others? And does patience have downsides? In simpler terms, Patience is the ability to tolerate delay, difficulty, or uncertainty without losing control immediately. It can come from self-control, trust, habit, cultural expectations, or the belief that waiting will lead to something worthwhile. But patience is not automatically good. Sometimes it helps people endure wisely; sometimes it becomes an excuse to accept unfairness or delay needed action. At the entry level, this helps students treat patience as a psychological and cultural skill, not just a slogan. At the section level, it returns directly to “Are we there yet?” as a question about emotion.

The official WSC examples

Would You Want to Know the Exact Waiting Time? begins from this official problem: Is it always better to know how long it’ll take you to get somewhere? If not, what are examples of times when it would be better not to know? In simpler terms, People often say they want certainty, but exact timing is not always comforting. A countdown can help when you need to plan a connection, deadline, or safety decision. But in emotional situations, knowing exactly how long you must wait can make every minute feel heavier. This entry asks whether information is useful because it is accurate, or because it helps people act and cope. The helpful distinction is practical waiting versus emotional waiting. At the entry level, this helps students see that knowledge changes feelings, not just decisions. At the section level, it returns to one of the year’s biggest questions: does knowing where you are in a journey always help?

Zeno and the Problem of Ever Getting There begins from this official problem: What if it takes us forever to get there? Consider the “dichotomy paradox” posed by the Greek philosopher Zeno. He observed that to walk across a thing—for instance, Greenland (which belongs to Denmark)—first you need to walk across half of it. In simpler terms, Zeno’s dichotomy paradox asks how anyone can ever arrive if every journey can be divided into more halfway steps forever. To cross a distance, you first reach halfway, then halfway of what remains, then halfway again, endlessly. Everyday life tells us motion obviously happens, but the paradox makes arrival feel logically puzzling. The linked article should be treated as content because it also introduces Zeno’s other motion puzzles and why later mathematics mattered. At the entry level, this helps students understand a famous paradox about motion and infinity. At the section level, it turns the theme’s simple question into a philosophical problem: what does “getting there” mean if the route can be divided forever?

8. Final synthesis

The big tensions

The main tension in Concluding Questions is that people want clear arrivals, but real life gives mixed routes. The section asks students to hold both sides at once: movement can be useful and costly, waiting can be empty and meaningful, and an ending can be official without feeling complete.

How the examples connect

The examples connect through a chain. Start with When Is It Wise to Let Go?, then compare it with Would You Want to Know the Exact Waiting Time?, Zeno and the Problem of Ever Getting There, and Patience: A Virtue, a Strategy, or a Burden?. From there, bring in the later entries to show how the same pressure changes when the scale moves from a person to a group, a city, a technology, a story, or a whole society.

Pitfalls to avoid

A basic answer treats each entry as a separate trivia item. A stronger answer asks what job the example does inside the section, how it changes the route question, and why WSC placed it near the other examples.

What alpacas should remember

Alpacas should remember the section as a set of tools, not as a list of facts. For every entry, be ready to say what is moving, what is delayed, who benefits, who pays, what sign of progress appears, and what kind of arrival is being questioned.
