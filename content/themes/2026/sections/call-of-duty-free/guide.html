Call of Duty-Free

Map of the guide

This guide keeps the app's route plan, but it reads Call of Duty-Free as one connected lesson for strong middle-school readers using B1 English. The guide slows down the examples so students can see the people, places, objects, dates, and choices inside each entry.

There are 5 official entries in this section: From Roadside Stops to Hotels, The Grand Tour: Travel as a Rite of Passage, The Golden Age of Travel… or Just a Golden Myth?, Traveling Differently: The Search for “Authentic” Tourism, and When Goods Travel: The World of Trade Hubs. Read them as a curriculum unit. Each entry gives one case, but the guide asks how the cases speak to each other.

The rewrite also checks the outside guide notes and resource packets, including Are We There Yet IGNITION, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 1, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 2, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 3, Intro Questions, other section notes. Those notes are used to add background, not to replace the official raw content.

The big route of the section

This section studies travel as commerce, comfort, status, and pressure. It asks what happens in the spaces around travel: hotels, grand tours, golden-age myths, authentic tourism, trade hubs, and duty-free zones.

The official WSC text gives the short version, but the linked sources and student explanations give the real working material. When an entry includes an article, video, poem, artwork, or historical case, treat that link as part of the syllabus.

Why these examples belong together

The examples belong together because travel is never only movement. It also creates markets, status symbols, waiting places, images of luxury, and conflicts between visitors and local people.

Core concepts explained from zero

These concepts are the basic tools for the section. They are simple on purpose. A student should be able to use each one in a short answer before moving to harder comparisons.

Chapter 1 - Duty-free as border pleasure

The idea

The chapter begins with When Goods Travel: The World of Trade Hubs. Its job is to turn one case into a tool: Goods rarely move in one simple jump; they depend on intermediate places that organize transit, storage, policy, and exchange.

The reading skill here is to connect a fact to a pattern. The main pattern is Movement Without Arrival, Infrastructure Shapes Behavior; the subject doorway is Economics & Trade, Geography / Human Geography. A student should be able to explain the case, then say what it teaches about Call of Duty-Free.

The official WSC examples

When Goods Travel: The World of Trade Hubs begins from this official problem: People aren’t the only things that travel; goods do too. Before long-distance shipping, a trader who did not want to confront the full length of a trade road might transport goods to or from an entrepôt. In simpler terms, Journeys are not only about people. Goods also move through routes, stops, transfer points, ports, warehouses, and trade policies. An entrepôt, a caravanserai, a waystation, a free-trade zone, or a transshipment point is not usually the final destination. It is a place where goods pause, change hands, avoid or delay duties, move from one vehicle to another, or connect one economy to another. At the entry level, this helps students understand that trade depends on routes and stopovers as much as human travel does. At the section level, it completes Call of Duty-Free by showing that movement includes goods, capital, customs, storage, and policy.

Chapter 2 - Inns, hotels, and temporary belonging

The idea

The chapter begins with From Roadside Stops to Hotels. Its job is to turn one case into a tool: Travel creates support spaces, and those spaces evolve as movement, comfort, commerce, and tourism change.

The reading skill here is to connect a fact to a pattern. The main pattern is Movement Without Arrival, Infrastructure Shapes Behavior; the subject doorway is History. A student should be able to explain the case, then say what it teaches about Call of Duty-Free.

The official WSC examples

From Roadside Stops to Hotels begins from this official problem: People on the road have historically needed places to park themselves too. With your team, explore the history of inns and hotels, including what might be the oldest hotel in the world. In simpler terms, Travelers have always needed support spaces: places to stop, sleep, feed animals, exchange news, wait safely, or continue a route the next day. Earlier forms such as caravanserais, monasteries, inns, taverns, and post houses were mainly tied to survival, trade, pilgrimage, or transport systems. A hotel became more specialized: a commercial place mainly designed to host travelers with comfort, privacy, service, and sometimes status. At the entry level, this helps students understand that hotels are part of a long infrastructure of movement. At the section level, it opens Call of Duty-Free by showing that travel depends not only on roads, ships, or vehicles, but also on stopover systems.

Chapter 3 - Travel as status

The idea

The chapter puts The Grand Tour: Travel as a Rite of Passage beside The Golden Age of Travel… or Just a Golden Myth?. The reading skill here is to connect a fact to a pattern. The official WSC examples

The Grand Tour: Travel as a Rite of Passage begins from this official problem: The rise of hotels coincided with the rise of tourism—travel for leisure, instead of for migration or business. Historians trace the roots of modern tourism to the grand tours of the 17th and 18th centuries, in which young aristocrats would travel across... In simpler terms, In the 17th and 18th centuries, many wealthy young British aristocrats took a long European journey called the Grand Tour. It was meant to complete their education by exposing them to language, art, classical ruins, manners, diplomacy, and elite social life. The typical route moved from Britain to France and then into Italy, with major stops such as Paris, Lyon, Venice, Florence, Rome, and Naples. At the entry level, this shows how travel became educational, cultural, and social rather than only practical. At the section level, it explains why hotels and tourism developed together: once travel became desirable in itself, systems grew to host and guide travelers.

The Golden Age of Travel… or Just a Golden Myth? begins from this official problem: Some people long for the so-called “Golden Age of Travel”: an era of opulent ocean liners, spacious flying boats, and wealthy travelers dressed to impress. They contrast this glowing picture with cramped modern airplanes and images of bad tourist behavior. In simpler terms, People often imagine a “Golden Age of Travel” when ocean liners were elegant, planes were spacious, and passengers dressed well. But that picture is only partly true. Older travel could be glamorous for the wealthy, but it was also expensive, less accessible, less safe, and not always comfortable. The real question is why the past looks better in memory. Once travel becomes ordinary, crowded, cheap enough for more people, and filmed constantly, its problems become more visible. At the entry level, this helps students separate nostalgia from historical reality. At the section level, it deepens Call of Duty-Free by showing that tourism is not only infrastructure and destinations, but also behavior and expectations.

The idea

The chapter begins with Traveling Differently: The Search for “Authentic” Tourism. Its job is to turn one case into a tool: Whether travel can create real learning and respect, or just turn new experiences into another product.

The reading skill here is to connect a fact to a pattern. The main pattern is Movement Without Arrival, Infrastructure Shapes Behavior; the subject doorway is Sociology, Media / Film. A student should be able to explain the case, then say what it teaches about Call of Duty-Free.

The official WSC examples

Traveling Differently: The Search for “Authentic” Tourism begins from this official problem: Although nothing beats a Jet2 Holiday, tourists are increasingly avoiding tours that shuffle everyone through the same overcrowded museums and restaurants in favor of more “authentic” travel experiences. In simpler terms, Many travelers now say they want something more “authentic” than the same overcrowded museums, restaurants, and landmarks. This creates a paradox. Social media can help people discover quieter places and more meaningful forms of travel, but it can also send huge crowds to those same places until they become trends. Heritage tourism, ecotourism, gastrotourism, agritourism, monastic retreats, voluntourism, edutourism, wellness retreats, and dark tourism all promise a different kind of journey. At the entry level, this helps students understand why niche tourism has grown and why “authenticity” is difficult to define. At the section level, it pushes Call of Duty-Free beyond hotels and transport into the meaning travelers want from travel.

8. Final synthesis

The big tensions

The main tension in Call of Duty-Free is that people want clear arrivals, but real life gives mixed routes. The section asks students to hold both sides at once: movement can be useful and costly, waiting can be empty and meaningful, and an ending can be official without feeling complete.

How the examples connect

The examples connect through a chain. Start with From Roadside Stops to Hotels, then compare it with The Grand Tour: Travel as a Rite of Passage, The Golden Age of Travel… or Just a Golden Myth?, and Traveling Differently: The Search for “Authentic” Tourism. From there, bring in the later entries to show how the same pressure changes when the scale moves from a person to a group, a city, a technology, a story, or a whole society.

Pitfalls to avoid

A basic answer treats each entry as a separate trivia item. A stronger answer asks what job the example does inside the section, how it changes the route question, and why WSC placed it near the other examples.

What alpacas should remember

Alpacas should remember the section as a set of tools, not as a list of facts. For every entry, be ready to say what is moving, what is delayed, who benefits, who pays, what sign of progress appears, and what kind of arrival is being questioned.
