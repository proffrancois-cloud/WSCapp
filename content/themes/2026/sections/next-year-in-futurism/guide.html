Next Year in Futurism

Map of the guide

This guide keeps the app's route plan, but it reads Next Year in Futurism as one connected lesson for strong middle-school readers using B1 English. The guide slows down the examples so students can see the people, places, objects, dates, and choices inside each entry.

There are 10 official entries in this section: Always Almost There, What 2018 Thought 2028 Would Look Like, Tesla’s Tower and the Meaning of “Soon”, Future Promises and the Problem of Timing, How Long Should Humans Try to Live?, Yesterday’s Tomorrow, Quantum Supremacy and the Fear of Q-Day, Can We Still Tell Human Writing from AI Writing?, What Would AGI Change?, and The Next Frontier Underground. Read them as a curriculum unit. Each entry gives one case, but the guide asks how the cases speak to each other.

The rewrite also checks the outside guide notes and resource packets, including Are We There Yet IGNITION, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 1, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 2, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 3, Intro Questions, other section notes. Those notes are used to add background, not to replace the official raw content.

The big route of the section

This section studies futures that feel close but keep moving away. It asks why people predict, promise, hype, remember, fear, and hope for technologies that may arrive late or not at all.

The official WSC text gives the short version, but the linked sources and student explanations give the real working material. When an entry includes an article, video, poem, artwork, or historical case, treat that link as part of the syllabus.

Why these examples belong together

The examples belong together because futurism is not just guessing. It is a cultural habit shaped by desire, money, fear, politics, old predictions, science, and the stories people tell about what will soon be possible.

Core concepts explained from zero

These concepts are the basic tools for the section. They are simple on purpose. A student should be able to use each one in a short answer before moving to harder comparisons.

FuturismThinking about possible futures and what they mean now.Ask who is predicting and what they want.

The official WSC examples

Always Almost There begins from this official problem: “Science has not yet mastered prophecy,” Neil Armstrong (the first man on the moon) once said. “We predict too much for the next year and yet far too little for the next ten. In simpler terms, Some technologies become permanent residents of the near future: famous enough to feel inevitable, but difficult enough to remain unfinished, niche, expensive, or socially awkward. Fusion power, flying cars, graphene, nanotechnology, life-extension dreams, and AGI all show the same pattern: the idea can be scientifically meaningful while the path to everyday use is delayed by engineering, cost, safety, regulation, scale, or exaggerated expectations. At the entry level, this helps students separate technical possibility from real-world arrival. At the section level, it opens futurism as a study of delayed promises, not just cool inventions.

What 2018 Thought 2028 Would Look Like begins from this official problem: With apologies to Mr. Armstrong, sometimes people also predict too much for the next ten. Consider these predictions for the year 2028, made in 2018. Are there any that haven’t come true yet but that you think still might in the next two years? In simpler terms, A 2018 forecast for 2028 is useful not because it gives a perfect map of the future, but because it reveals what investors and technology thinkers thought was accelerating at that moment. The article predicts a new mobile form factor, major autonomous-car adoption, a huge freelance workforce, longer lives, security drones, mainstream vegetarianism, cashless payment, solar growth, enhanced athletes, VR addiction, and more Brexit talks. At the entry level, this teaches students to evaluate predictions after time has passed. At the section level, it gives a concrete forecast to test rather than abstract futurism.

The official WSC examples

Future Promises and the Problem of Timing begins from this official problem: Tesla is far from the only person to have declared that “it will soon be possible…” Sentences that begin with this and similar phrases (e.g., “In a few years, everyone will…”) almost always make ambitious predictions about the near future. In simpler terms, Futurist language often sounds precise while remaining vague. Phrases like “soon,” “in a few years,” and “everyone will” turn uncertainty into confidence. This entry trains students to read prediction language carefully and ask what exactly is being promised: technical possibility, mass adoption, cheap access, legal permission, or cultural acceptance. Tesla’s Tower and the Meaning of “Soon” begins from this official problem: “It will soon be possible to transmit wireless messages all over the world so simply that any individual can own and operate his own apparatus,” the inventor Nikola Tesla told the New York Times in 1904. In simpler terms, Tesla’s 1904 prediction of personal wireless communication sounds astonishingly close to the smartphone, but Wardenclyffe shows why being visionary is not the same as arriving. Tesla imagined global wireless communication and power, and the tower was meant to be a prototype for a worldwide system. The project failed because of funding, timing, technical ambition, and lack of institutional support. At the entry level, this shows that a prediction can point in the right direction while still failing in its actual form. At the section level, it develops futurism as a problem of timing, funding, infrastructure, and credibility.

The Next Frontier Underground begins from this official problem: “Because it’s there,” the British explorer George Mallory once responded, when asked why he wanted to climb Mt. Everest. (It’s not clear if he ever got there. In simpler terms, The Kola Superdeep Borehole reminds students that the future is not always above us in space or ahead of us in computers. Sometimes the next frontier is below our feet. During the Cold War, the United States and Soviet Union competed to drill deep into Earth’s crust; the Soviet project reached more than 12 kilometers before heat, technical limits, and political collapse ended the effort. The entry asks whether rivalry helps humanity move faster, or whether it can distort exploration into prestige competition. At the entry level, this shows that frontiers are chosen and culturally framed. At the section level, it closes futurism by asking not just what we can chase, but why we chase it. At the year-theme level, it connects to progress and prediction because getting “there” depends on values, not only distance.

8. Final synthesis

The big tensions

The main tension in Next Year in Futurism is that people want clear arrivals, but real life gives mixed routes. The section asks students to hold both sides at once: movement can be useful and costly, waiting can be empty and meaningful, and an ending can be official without feeling complete.

How the examples connect

The examples connect through a chain. Start with Always Almost There, then compare it with What 2018 Thought 2028 Would Look Like, Tesla’s Tower and the Meaning of “Soon”, and Future Promises and the Problem of Timing. From there, bring in the later entries to show how the same pressure changes when the scale moves from a person to a group, a city, a technology, a story, or a whole society.

Pitfalls to avoid

A basic answer treats each entry as a separate trivia item. A stronger answer asks what job the example does inside the section, how it changes the route question, and why WSC placed it near the other examples.

What alpacas should remember

Alpacas should remember the section as a set of tools, not as a list of facts. For every entry, be ready to say what is moving, what is delayed, who benefits, who pays, what sign of progress appears, and what kind of arrival is being questioned.
