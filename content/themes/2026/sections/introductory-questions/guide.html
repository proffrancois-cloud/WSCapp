Introductory Questions

Map of the guide

This guide keeps the app's route plan, but it reads Introductory Questions as one connected lesson for strong middle-school readers using B1 English. The guide slows down the examples so students can see the people, places, objects, dates, and choices inside each entry.

There are 6 official entries in this section: To Consider Before Diving In, Frank Sinatra, My Way, The Lord of the Rings: Nice Trilogy, Strange Ending Experience?, Simba, Teenagers, and Life in the In-Between, Technologies That Seemed Close but Never Arrived, and Things We Commit To but Do Not Finish. Read them as a curriculum unit. Each entry gives one case, but the guide asks how the cases speak to each other.

The rewrite also checks the outside guide notes and resource packets, including Are We There Yet IGNITION, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 1, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 2, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 3, Intro Questions, other section notes. Those notes are used to add background, not to replace the official raw content.

The big route of the section

This opening section teaches the big question of the year. Being 'there' can mean arriving at a place, finishing a task, reaching a life stage, or deciding that the journey is no longer worth the cost.

The official WSC text gives the short version, but the linked sources and student explanations give the real working material. When an entry includes an article, video, poem, artwork, or historical case, treat that link as part of the syllabus.

Why these examples belong together

The examples belong together because they all make arrival less simple. A song can look back at life, a film can make an ending feel too long, a child can want adult power too early, and a future technology can stay almost ready for decades.

Core concepts explained from zero

These concepts are the basic tools for the section. They are simple on purpose. A student should be able to use each one in a short answer before moving to harder comparisons.

ConceptSimple meaningHow to use it
ArrivalReaching a point that people agree to call the end.Use it carefully, because not every arrival feels complete.
ThresholdA line after which the situation changes.Look for moments when going back becomes hard or impossible.
Near-endingA moment that feels like the end before the real end arrives.Use it with stories, projects, politics, and climate warnings.
Point of no returnThe moment when a route can no longer be reversed easily.Ask who notices this point and who misses it.

Chapter 1 - Different kinds of there

The idea

The chapter begins with To Consider Before Diving In. Its job is to turn one case into a tool: To show that progress questions reach beyond physical travel.

The reading skill here is to connect a fact to a pattern. The main pattern is Movement Without Arrival, Delayed Arrival; the subject doorway is Philosophy, Psychology. A student should be able to explain the case, then say what it teaches about Introductory Questions.

The official WSC examples

To Consider Before Diving In begins from this official problem: The journey of a thousand miles begins with a single step, but how does it end? Have you ever asked someone, "Are we there yet?" If so, whom were you asking—and where were you going? Did you ever get there? In simpler terms, This opening entry is the map for the whole section. It starts with the old Laozi/Tao Te Ching idea that a huge journey begins from where you are standing, then asks what happens after the first step: how do we know we arrived, whether it was worth it, and whether an ending is physical, emotional, official, or personal? The road-trip question “Are we there yet?” is useful because it shows impatience, dependence on someone else’s route, and uncertainty about distance. At the entry level, this teaches students how to read the theme: arrival is not always simple, and endings can change how a journey feels. At the section level, it gives the introduction a clear job: to connect everyday questions to the larger WSC route.

The official WSC examples

Frank Sinatra, My Way begins from this official problem: "And now, the end is near," croons Frank Sinatra. But how do we know when we're getting to an ending—or to a point of no return? In simpler terms, “My Way” gives the section a concrete model of a person looking back from the edge of an ending. The song was adapted by Paul Anka from the French “Comme d’habitude,” first recorded by Claude François in 1967, but Anka changed the meaning from a failing relationship into a self-directed life review for Frank Sinatra’s 1969 version. That shift matters: the same melody becomes a different kind of ending depending on the story attached to it. At the entry level, this turns “the end” into a moment of reflection, not only a finish. At the section level, it gives the introduction an emotional example of how endings can make people review the route behind them.

The Lord of the Rings: Nice Trilogy, Strange Ending Experience? begins from this official problem: In 2003, millions of people gathered at theaters to watch the final Lord of the Rings movie. They watched it for a long time: three and a half hours. In simpler terms, The Return of the King shows that a story can end in layers instead of one clean stop. In theaters in 2003, viewers could not glance at a progress bar, so every possible ending felt like it might be the last. At the entry level, this shows that endings are partly experienced through pacing, expectation, and information. At the section level, it connects progress to storytelling: the journey through a movie can feel different depending on whether the audience knows how close they are to the end.

The official WSC examples

Simba, Teenagers, and Life in the In-Between begins from this official problem: "I just can’t wait to be king," complains Simba. He is not quite one thing, not quite another. Not everyone is the heir to the throne, but we do all spend time as teenagers, no longer children but not yet adults. In simpler terms, “I Just Can’t Wait to Be King” comes from Disney’s The Lion King, with music by Elton John and lyrics by Tim Rice. Young Simba imagines kingship as freedom: no orders, no supervision, everyone admiring him. Zazu’s interruptions matter because they reveal what Simba does not yet understand: power also means responsibility, patience, and care for others. That is why the song fits adolescence. At the entry level, this gives students a familiar example of liminality. At the section level, it links the introduction to Going Pains and the wider theme of thresholds.

Technologies That Seemed Close but Never Arrived begins from this official problem: Are there lessons we can learn from technologies that once seemed about to arrive—nuclear-powered cars, food pills, 3D televisions, and many more—but haven’t yet? In simpler terms, This entry is about futures that looked close because they matched the dreams of their moment, then failed when reality pushed back. Nuclear-powered cars came from the Atomic Age optimism of the late 1950s and early 1960s, with concept models such as the Ford Nucleon, Studebaker-Packard Astral, Arbel Symétric, and Seattle-ite XXI promising extreme range or futuristic travel. At the entry level, this teaches students to question predictions instead of simply believing them. At the section level, it gives the introduction a bridge into futurism.

Chapter 4 - Life as a journey, with limits

The idea

The chapter begins with Things We Commit To but Do Not Finish. Its job is to turn one case into a tool: Incomplete journeys can come from structure, pressure, and motivation.

The reading skill here is to connect a fact to a pattern. The main pattern is Delayed Arrival, Movement Without Arrival; the subject doorway is Psychology. A student should be able to explain the case, then say what it teaches about Introductory Questions.

The official WSC examples

Things We Commit To but Do Not Finish begins from this official problem: The truth is that sometimes we’ll never get there at all. Most videogames are left unfinished; nearly half of those who start university don’t get a degree. In simpler terms, This entry turns “Are we there yet?” into the harder question: why do we start routes we never complete? Video games make this visible because many players begin large games but never reach the final mission; analyses point to older and busier players, too many games competing for attention, social media and app distractions, and the rise of multiplayer modes that reward short sessions more than long endings. At the entry level, this helps students separate starting from finishing. At the section level, it gives the introduction a realistic counterpoint: some routes do not end in arrival.

7. Final synthesis

The big tensions

The main tension in Introductory Questions is that people want clear arrivals, but real life gives mixed routes. The section asks students to hold both sides at once: movement can be useful and costly, waiting can be empty and meaningful, and an ending can be official without feeling complete.

How the examples connect

The examples connect through a chain. Start with To Consider Before Diving In, then compare it with Frank Sinatra, My Way, The Lord of the Rings: Nice Trilogy, Strange Ending Experience?, and Simba, Teenagers, and Life in the In-Between. From there, bring in the later entries to show how the same pressure changes when the scale moves from a person to a group, a city, a technology, a story, or a whole society.

Pitfalls to avoid

A basic answer treats each entry as a separate trivia item. A stronger answer asks what job the example does inside the section, how it changes the route question, and why WSC placed it near the other examples.

What alpacas should remember

Alpacas should remember the section as a set of tools, not as a list of facts. For every entry, be ready to say what is moving, what is delayed, who benefits, who pays, what sign of progress appears, and what kind of arrival is being questioned.
