The End is Nearish

Map of the guide

This guide keeps the app's route plan, but it reads The End is Nearish as one connected lesson for strong middle-school readers using B1 English. The guide slows down the examples so students can see the people, places, objects, dates, and choices inside each entry.

There are 10 official entries in this section: When Power Is Supposed to Be Temporary, Lame Ducks and Fading Power, If No One Knew Who Was Next, The Clock That Measures Doom, Why People Keep Predicting the End, Renaissance Visions of the End, Modern Apocalypses and New Fears, A Soundtrack for the End, The Point of No Return, and Profiting from Disaster. Read them as a curriculum unit. Each entry gives one case, but the guide asks how the cases speak to each other.

The rewrite also checks the outside guide notes and resource packets, including Are We There Yet IGNITION, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 1, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 2, CORNUCOPIA NOTES 3, Intro Questions, other section notes. Those notes are used to add background, not to replace the official raw content.

The big route of the section

This section studies the strange time before an ending. The end may be scheduled, predicted, feared, symbolic, profitable, or already beginning while people still act as if life is normal.

The official WSC text gives the short version, but the linked sources and student explanations give the real working material. When an entry includes an article, video, poem, artwork, or historical case, treat that link as part of the syllabus.

Why these examples belong together

The examples belong together because they all ask what happens when people believe an end is close. Leaders lose power, clocks warn the public, artists imagine disaster, and companies may even find ways to profit from fear.

Core concepts explained from zero

These concepts are the basic tools for the section. They are simple on purpose. A student should be able to use each one in a short answer before moving to harder comparisons.

Chapter 1 - Political endings before power is gone

The idea

The chapter moves from When Power Is Supposed to Be Temporary to Lame Ducks and Fading Power, and If No One Knew Who Was Next. The route is not random: it starts with this point: It is limited power meant to bridge a transition. Then it tests that idea through Lame Ducks and Fading Power and ends by asking how If No One Knew Who Was Next changes the pattern.

The reading skill here is to connect a fact to a pattern. The main pattern is Narrated Endings, Thresholds & Irreversibility, Managing Uncertainty; the subject doorway is Politics & Government / Global Politics, History. A student should be able to explain the case, then say what it teaches about The End is Nearish.

The official WSC examples

When Power Is Supposed to Be Temporary begins from this official problem: In November 2025, the Supreme Court of Bangladesh restored the practice of caretaker governments. In most countries with parliamentary systems, when an election is about to take place, a caretaker government runs things until the election is over. In simpler terms, A caretaker government is a government for the in-between. During an election or transfer of power, leaders may remain in office to keep the country running, but they are expected to avoid major new decisions that should belong to the next fully empowered government. The Bangladesh case in the raw text makes the issue concrete: caretaker systems can be used to protect election fairness, but they can also become political battlegrounds about who should control the transition. At the entry level, this explains how some political systems manage power before a new government fully arrives. At the section level, it opens The End Is Nearish by showing that endings in politics are rarely clean: power can become temporary before it disappears.

Lame Ducks and Fading Power begins from this official problem: The United States has no caretaker governments (neither does Venezuela), but it does limit its presidents to two four-year terms in office. As their second term begins to run out, they inevitably find themselves losing power and influence. In simpler terms, A lame duck leader is still in office, but everyone knows the end is coming, so influence starts slipping before the official finish line. In the United States, the two-term presidential limit helps create this problem because a second-term president cannot run again. The real issue is a trade-off: term limits can protect a system from permanent leadership, but they can also make a leader weaker before the term is actually over. A lame duck has office without the same future leverage. At the entry level, this helps students see that political endings can begin before the calendar says they have arrived. At the section level, it extends The End Is Nearish from temporary governments to fading authority.

If No One Knew Who Was Next begins from this official problem: Queen Elizabeth I’s solution was to delay naming her successor. “I know the inconstancy of the people of England,” she is said to have told the Scottish ambassador, “How they ever mislike the present government and have their eyes fixed upon the person who... In simpler terms, Elizabeth I delayed naming a successor because succession is not just a legal detail; it changes where people place their loyalty. If everyone knows who comes next, the future leader can start shaping the present before the old reign is over. But if no one knows, the current leader may preserve authority while creating uncertainty, rivalry, and fear about what happens after the end. Elizabeth I’s succession strategy shows uncertainty as a political tool. At the entry level, this shows that transitions can begin in people’s minds before they happen officially. At the section level, it deepens The End Is Nearish by asking when an ending truly starts: at death, departure, announcement, or rumor.

The official WSC examples

The Clock That Measures Doom begins from this official problem: Someday there may be nobody left to lead. Consider the Doomsday Clock, which tries to measure how close we are to the end of human civilization. Discuss with your team: how accurate do you think it is, and in what ways, if any, is it a helpful tool? In simpler terms, The Doomsday Clock is not a machine that calculates the exact end of civilization. It is a symbolic warning created by the Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists to make global catastrophic risk visible. The current official links matter because they show the clock is updated as a judgment about changing dangers such as nuclear weapons, climate change, AI, biosecurity, and international conflict. Its strength is also its weakness: it gets attention, but it can look more mathematically precise than it really is. At the entry level, this helps students separate symbolic measurement from literal prediction. At the section level, it introduces apocalypse as something people try to communicate through public images and countdowns.

Why People Keep Predicting the End begins from this official problem: Midnight was coming. The world was on fire and people were dying of a terrible illness. Surely, the end of the world was near—or so thought Londoners in 1666. It was also near in 1844, 1910, 1988, 2000 and 2012. In simpler terms, Doomsday predictions often appear when people already feel that the world is unstable. The 1666 link shows Londoners connecting plague, fire, religious conflict, and the number 666 into a story of final judgment. Later examples did the same with comets, calendars, computers, or physics experiments. These predictions fail again and again, but they spread because they turn confusing fear into a simple story with a date, a cause, and a dramatic ending. At the entry level, this helps students compare failed apocalypses as social and emotional patterns. At the section level, it expands The End Is Nearish from official risk warnings to viral belief.

The idea

The official WSC examples

The Point of No Return begins from this official problem: Chaos, then catastrophe. Researchers have found that one of the signs that an ecosystem is about to collapse is that things become more volatile—for instance, the amount of chlorophyll in a lake rapidly spikes and plunges. In simpler terms, A point of no return is a threshold after which change becomes extremely difficult or impossible to undo. The links are central because they show warning signs, not just metaphors: ecosystems may become more volatile before collapse, climate change can increase unstable extreme temperature swings, and even flight turbulence can become one everyday sign of a changing atmosphere. The hardest question is not only whether a threshold exists, but how people should act if they fear they are approaching it. At the entry level, this connects collapse to evidence and warning signs. At the section level, it turns apocalyptic thinking away from fantasy and toward measurable real-world systems.

Profiting from Disaster begins from this official problem: Some investors are looking for business opportunities around the warming of the Earth. Explore some of those opportunities, then discuss with your team: should people be allowed to profit from rising temperatures? In simpler terms, Profiting from disaster is not as simple as saying all crisis businesses are bad. People need tools, shelters, insurance, adaptation systems, and climate technologies. But the Politico/E&E link raises a sharper problem: if investors can make billions from risky climate interventions such as solar geoengineering, their financial success may depend on climate failure becoming bad enough to create demand. The ethical question is whether preparation reduces harm or quietly creates incentives to accept disaster. At the entry level, this helps students distinguish useful preparation from harmful incentives. At the section level, it gives The End Is Nearish a contemporary economic ending: apocalypse becomes a market opportunity.

The official WSC examples

Renaissance Visions of the End begins from this official problem: The four horsemen of the apocalypse, fires raging across more than California: the Book of Revelation has long offered artists a rich source of imagery for the end of the world. In simpler terms, Renaissance apocalypse art does not simply show destruction. It turns the end of the world into a visual system of judgment: some figures rise, some fall, some are saved, and some are overwhelmed. The links matter because each artwork imagines the final threshold differently. Dürer makes the horsemen feel fast and violent; Botticelli connects apocalypse to renewal; Michelangelo builds a vast moral drama; Bruegel makes death spread across everyday society. At the entry level, this helps students read apocalypse as visual storytelling about morality, fear, and destiny. At the section level, it gives The End Is Nearish an art-history dimension.

Modern Apocalypses and New Fears begins from this official problem: Consider the more recent apocalypses depicted below. How have they changed as the world has moved toward industrial and digital anxiety—and what specific fears are these artists inviting us to explore? In simpler terms, Modern apocalyptic works often move away from purely religious judgment and toward fears that humans create: empire collapsing, industry failing, war destroying bodies, fascism spreading, cities emptying, and digital media imagining survival after breakdown. The links are the core content because each work shows a different fear of its time. Together, they ask whether the end of the world now looks less like divine punishment and more like the result of human systems losing control. At the entry level, this helps students compare how apocalypse changes with history. At the section level, it moves The End Is Nearish from sacred endings to modern anxieties.

A Soundtrack for the End begins from this official problem: The end of the world can have a soundtrack, too. Consider the musical selections below. What were the circumstances around each one’s creation, and what techniques do they use to achieve their messages and moods? In simpler terms, Apocalypse can be heard as well as seen. Messiaen makes the end feel suspended, spiritual, and outside ordinary time; Black Sabbath makes it heavy, electric, and nuclear; R.E.M. makes it fast, crowded, and almost funny while still anxious. The links are essential because the lesson is in the sound: different rhythms, textures, silences, and vocal styles create different emotional versions of “the end.” Sound changes the shape of apocalypse. At the entry level, this helps students understand apocalyptic mood through music. At the section level, it broadens The End Is Nearish beyond paintings and prophecies into sound.

8. Final synthesis

The big tensions

The main tension in The End is Nearish is that people want clear arrivals, but real life gives mixed routes. The section asks students to hold both sides at once: movement can be useful and costly, waiting can be empty and meaningful, and an ending can be official without feeling complete.

How the examples connect

The examples connect through a chain. Start with When Power Is Supposed to Be Temporary, then compare it with Lame Ducks and Fading Power, If No One Knew Who Was Next, and The Clock That Measures Doom. From there, bring in the later entries to show how the same pressure changes when the scale moves from a person to a group, a city, a technology, a story, or a whole society.

Pitfalls to avoid

A basic answer treats each entry as a separate trivia item. A stronger answer asks what job the example does inside the section, how it changes the route question, and why WSC placed it near the other examples.

What alpacas should remember

Alpacas should remember the section as a set of tools, not as a list of facts. For every entry, be ready to say what is moving, what is delayed, who benefits, who pays, what sign of progress appears, and what kind of arrival is being questioned.
